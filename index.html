01 · learn

It starts with files you already have.

No forms, no data entry project. Sales exports, count sheets and supplier invoices are replayed into one brain per venue — and the venues genuinely diverge: one runs flat all week, the next peaks Saturdays, the third Sundays. Each learns its own rhythm; the group brain sits over all three.

the learned patterns are inspectable in the app — every one names the data it was learned from and what it drives.

02 · refill

Orders start from the forecast. You keep the OK.

Every night: forecast tomorrow's demand from the learned rhythm, subtract what's on hand, respect pack sizes, price each line from the latest invoice actually paid. The draft lands consolidated per supplier; you amend or approve — and every amend is a correction the brain learns from. When the delivery lands, the invoice is photographed at the door, read line-by-line, and the prices feed the next draft.

approve is idempotent — a double-tap can never order twice. the invoice echo is verbatim: what the brain read is what you see.

03 · doubt

It doubts its own books — item by item, venue by venue.

Derived stock is a claim, not a fact — so the brain scores its own uncertainty per ingredient: money at stake × time since a human confirmed × how erratically the item moves. Each venue gets its own queue, priced by its own sales — Downtown's beef-patty exposure is €192, Beach's €136 — and a count at one venue never silences doubt at another.

this ranking is what decides the ONE question the floor gets asked (next moment). answered items leave the queue; the rest re-rank.

04 · ask

Front of house sees one question. That's the whole app.

Nobody on the floor opens a dashboard. A quiet pill sits in the corner; when the brain's doubt crosses a line, it asks one thing — typed out like a person, answered with a number or a voice note. The answer corrects the books, grades the brain's own derivation, and the pill recedes.

5 open questions across 38 tracked items in the demo world — answer one and watch the count drop. fewer questions every month is the point.

05 · recap

At close, it tells you what it got wrong — and files what you tell it.

The evening dialogue closes the loop: the brain owns its day out loud — inside or outside its own band — and asks what it couldn't see. A waste line or a correction typed back is parsed and filed, not lost in a chat log. And every graded call lands on a decision ledger (27 in the demo world: forecasts, drift flags, spot-checks, an order draft) that a replay harness turns into a published report card — worst miss as the headline.

n is small and the page says so — 13 banded forecasts is a calibration baseline, not a brag. the discipline is the product; the number just has to be honest.

what this demo is — exactly

Honest about its own seams.

  • Every number you scrolled past is real engine output — captured byte-for-byte from the production API running over a seeded, fictional tenant ("Burger House", world frozen at 2026-05-31). Nothing on this page was composed by hand.
  • The app behind the buttons is the real frontend, built statically: reads replay those captures; the five write loops (counts, sends, corrections, invoice upload) are simulated in-browser against the same rules the backend applies.
  • The invoice "OCR" in this demo assembles its extract from the order book — the production extractor is an LLM behind a fail-loud seam, not shipped in a static page. The receiving pill's "file a sample one" button makes the flow testable: the sample carries one price 5% over last-paid, so you can watch it land on the draft order and raise the price flag (which the nightly run raises in production).
  • The report card says "small n" because n is small. 13 banded forecasts is a calibration baseline. The claim is the discipline — forecast, band, grade, publish — not the percentage.
  • The platform's source is private; this page and the built bundle are the showcase. The logic is documented in the README.
