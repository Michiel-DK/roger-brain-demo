01 · learn

It learns each venue — then a group brain sits over them.

No forms, no data-entry project. Sales exports, count sheets and supplier invoices are replayed into one brain per venue — and the venues genuinely diverge: Downtown runs flat all week, Riverside peaks Saturdays at lunch, Beach leans Sunday. Each learns its own rhythm.

Above them, a group brain shares what should be shared — pars, prices, the corrections you make — without flattening the differences each site earned.

every learned pattern names the data it came from and what it drives — nothing here is a claim the data can't back.

02 · stock levels

It keeps Downtown's levels — and flags what's running lean.

What's on the book against the level this venue's own demand justifies. That level isn't a flat par you typed once: it's set from how much the item actually swings day to day — a steady item sits tight to its usage, a jumpy one carries more margin.

The bar and its line say it at a glance; the reasoning is one tap away. Open why this level and the brain shows its work — how erratic the item is, and how many days it learned from.

the level re-learns as the venue's demand moves — it isn't a number anyone re-types.

03 · orders

The order follows the levels — you keep the OK.

The lean items set tonight's draft. The brain forecasts tomorrow, subtracts what's on hand, respects pack sizes and prices each line from the last invoice actually paid — then lands it consolidated per supplier, with a "who needs it" strip so you see which site drives the spend.

Change any line right there — bump the beef, drop a line — then approve. Nothing sends without you, and every change is logged so the next draft comes back closer to your call.

approve is idempotent — a double-tap can never place the order twice.

04 · the door · a pill

When the delivery lands, the door reads its own invoice.

Nobody types it in. The receiving pill — the same one-thing-at-a-time surface the floor uses — asks one thing: photo the invoice. It reads the lines, echoes back exactly what it read, and files the prices straight onto the books, ready to price the next draft.

the echo is verbatim: what the brain read is what you see. if it can't read a line it says so and prices nothing — it never invents a number. → watch it read ↓

05 · the floor · a pill

It spot-checks itself — starting where doubt is highest.

Derived stock is a claim, not a fact — so the brain ranks its own uncertainty per item: money at stake × how long since anyone actually counted it. The top of the queue becomes one quick question on the floor's pill — a number back, and it corrects itself.

it starts with the item it doubts most, then works down. answer one and it leaves the queue — fewer questions as it learns. no dashboard, one thing at a time.

06 · recap

One end-of-day chat, and tomorrow adapts.

The brain gives the day back in a line and asks the one thing it can't read from the numbers — how it actually felt. Answer in a voice note on the way out, and it doesn't sit in a chat log: a busy night lifts tomorrow's forecast, a tray of waste trims tomorrow's par.

no forms — a recap and a voice note. what you say changes the plan for tomorrow, right in front of you.

about this demo

A real engine on a made-up restaurant.

  • "Burger House" is fictional — the engine behind it isn't. Every figure you scrolled past is real output from the same system that would run on your venues, over a seeded demo history. Nothing on this page was mocked up by hand.
  • The invoice reader never invents a number. It echoes back exactly what it read; if it can't read a line, it says so and prices nothing.
  • It runs on a small demo history, so the numbers are illustrative. What's real is the loop — learn, order, check, recap, learn again — which runs the same once it's on your data. The logic is documented in the README.
